TIP #2: Make Your Workouts Seasonal

Embrace the change of seasons and take advantage of activities that you can only do in fall or winter! 

  • Go on a fall hike and view all the gorgeous foliage.
  • Try rock climbing.
  • Walk around a corn maze or pumpkin patch with your family.

TIP #3: Try Something Totally New

Sometimes a change of routine is all you need to boost motivation.

If you have been doing all your gym lifts alone, recruit a gym buddy! Try switching up your cardio by skipping your solo runs and joining a group fitness class instead. 

Another option is to allow yourself to slow down! If you have been crushing it all spring and summer with intense training and hardcore workouts, it may actually be time to take it down a notch. 

Going full force 365 can cause your stress hormone cortisol to increase. This can lead to a host of drawbacks, including weight gain, muscle loss, poor moods, fatigue, and occasional issues sleeping! 

We aren’t saying you should become lazy! But if you have been crushing it 24/7 all spring and summer, your body (and mind) may need to slow down.

Use the next few months to embrace a slower routine. 

  • Commit to daily yoga.
  • Focus on becoming more flexible.
  • Switch to low or moderate-intensity cardio, instead of HIIT.
  • Try out some bodyweight workouts instead of heavy lifting.

Giving your body a couple of months to recover might even make you more responsive when you decide to kick things up again come spring/summer!

TIP #4: Stay Accountable


The best way to stick to a plan is to have someone that you report back to. This could be a friend, family member, a social media community, or even a coach. 

Including someone else, or even just telling someone else about your health and fitness goals and asking them to check in with you to make sure you are sticking to the plan, is a great way to keep the promises you make to yourself.

If you prefer to fly solo, it helps to create visuals. Make a vision board, create a daily checklist you can mark off after each workout, or keep an accomplishment journal.

TIP #5: Set A Spring Goal

March may seem like a long time away, but it will be back before you know it, and 6 months is a long time to make significant changes. 

Setting a goal is a great way to encourage yourself to push past discomforts and temptation, but there is a science to goal setting.

The key to creating goals that stick .. be REALISTIC and SPECIFIC!

Let’s start with reality. You should always focus on one goal at a time and make sure you are being honest with yourself if that goal is really attainable.

If you know that your schedule will never allow you to work out 6 days a week, why would you choose a program that requires you to do that?

If you know it’s not healthy to attempt to lose 10 pounds a week, why would you aim that high?

If you love carbs, why would you go on a KETO diet? 

Doing these things will essentially set yourself up for failure. Set attainable goals that fit YOUR lifestyle. You can always bump things up if your schedule allows, or you notice you aren’t pushing yourself hard enough. Just keep in mind that being realistic isn’t the same as not believing in yourself or making excuses. 

Moving on to being specific. The more specific you are about your goals, the more likely you will be to reach them. 

It may seem like it’s good enough to say things like .. I want to look good, lose weight, or get ripped! But you have to dig deeper than that!! 

Wanting to “lose weight,” for example, is an excellent place to start. But ask yourself what that means to you?

If you expand on that and say — “I want to lose 10 pounds of fat. I will do this by not eating any sweets and doing the V Shred MOVE program for 30 consecutive days. The more specific (and attainable) your goals are, the more likely you will be to not give up!

How Many Calories Should I Eat to Lose Weight?

The amount of calories you need to eat depends on a number of different factors including your age, weight, body, gender, and activity level. That being said, each person has unique individual attributes that are the basis of caloric needs and consumption.

High Protein Diet Plans You Should Look Into to Get Cut

 

1. Avoid a Lack of Protein

A lack of protein can wreak havoc on your body in a number of ways. In particular, if you’re on a low-calorie diet plan and you don’t get enough protein, your body starts to shed muscle instead of fat.

You may also suffer from fatigue, depression, anxiety, and anemia (low iron). Your body relies on nine essential amino acids, and when you don’t get enough protein, you experience a deficiency of these amino acids.

Your exact symptoms can vary depending on which amino acids your body is missing.

2. Try These High-Protein Foods


Some of the best high-protein foods include lean grass-fed beef, organic chicken, salmon, eggs, bone broth, and goat cheese. When comparing different foods, take into account how much protein they offer based on their weight.

For instance, 3 ounces of lean beef has about 22 grams of protein, and three ounces of goat cheese has 21 grams of protein. In contrast with other foods, you may eat 3 ounces but get a lot less protein.

Also, remember to look at the food’s other advantages such as vitamins, minerals, healing benefits, pH levels, and so on. Most importantly, pay attention to how different foods make you feel.

If you get an energy boost after eating a certain food while another makes you feel sluggish, you need probably to focus on the first option.

3. Focus on Lean Proteins

As a general rule of thumb, you need one gram of lean protein for every pound of body weight. For instance, if you weigh 180 pounds, you need 180 grams of lean protein per day.

But depending on your weight loss goals, you may need to adjust that amount. This helps you to keep your lean muscle mass while losing unwanted fat.

In contrast, if you eat proteins with lots of fats, you will struggle to meet your weight loss goals.

4. Don’t Avoid High-Protein Diets Because You’re Vegan

When many people think of high-protein diets, they assume that you have to eat meat. Luckily, that isn’t the case.

If you’re vegetarian or vegan, you can still enjoy a high protein diet. Lentils, black beans, and almonds are all extremely high-protein options.

You may also want to eat chia seeds, hummus (chickpeas), and soybeans as well as a variety of other nuts and beans. This type of diet is much healthier than simply trying to get by on sugars and carbs.

To ensure you get all the amino acids you need, consider focusing on complete proteins. On their own, foods such as quinoa, buckwheat, and soy are complete proteins. But in many other cases, you need to pair two foods to get a complete protein.

This includes iconic pairings such as rice and beans and hummus and pita.

5. Minimize the Carbohydrates You Eat

On the flip side of the coin, you need to avoid excess carbs when you’re on high protein diet plans. For a low-carb diet, stay away from simple sugars and basic carbs.

6. Remember to Hydrate


Water is essential when you’re on a high-protein, low-carb diet. Water helps your cells to function. It also lubricates joints, minimizes dry mouth, and promotes cardiovascular health.

On the outside, it keeps your skin looking supple and youthful. Inside of your body, it helps to filter waste. Additionally, if you don’t drink enough water, that can prevent your kidneys from functioning correctly, and your kidneys play a key role in high-protein diets.

7. Stay Away from One-Size-Fits-All Diets

Over the years, a number of high-protein diets have become popular. They include the following:

  • Protein Power Diet — Developed in the 1990s, this diet involves over 54 percent fat, and it requires you to avoid carbs as well as fruits and starchy vegetables.
  • The Zone Diet — Also from the mid-1990s, this diet created by Dr. Barry Sears consists of 40 percent carbohydrates, 30 percent protein, and 30 percent fat. It focuses on having frequent low calories snacks.
  • The Atkins Diet — Focused on proteins and fat with a minimum of carbs, this diet was popular around the turn of the millennium. Although many people reported losing weight, the excessive amount of fat associated with this diet can increase the risk of kidney damage and heart disease or other issues.
  • South Beach Diet — Debuted in 2003, the South Beach Diet allows more carbs than many other high-protein diets, but it restricts fruits and carrots because of their position on the glycemic index.
  • Sugar Busters Diet — Unlike most high-protein diets, this plan is over half carbohydrates, but it minimizes simple starches such as white rice, white flour, potatoes, corn, and even carrots.
  • Stillman Diet — One of the diets recommending the highest levels of protein, this diet pushes you toward a plan that is 64 percent protein, 33 percent fat, and just 3 percent carbohydrates.

Although some people have had success with these diets, the main issue is that they offer a one-size-fits all approach to dieting. You need a plan that is customized to your size, your body type, and your objectives.

In other cases, you may find that you prefer to just stick to your diet because cheating once can turn into cheating all the time.

As you settle into your new diet, cravings are inevitable. So that you don’t yield to them, you need to be prepared.
